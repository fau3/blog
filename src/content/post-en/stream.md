Starting from Tatsuro Yamashita's perspective, let's talk about a question we face every day but rarely think about seriously.

Recently, I read some materials about Tatsuro Yamashita, and a quote of his left a deep impression on me. The gist was: those who were not involved in the creation process freely distribute songs on streaming media and profit from them. This is a victory for the market, but it has nothing to do with music.

I am a steadfast streaming user. Spotify and NetEase Cloud Music are always active on my phone and computer. If Tatsuro Yamashita's music were available on streaming services, I would definitely be the first to listen. But this does not stop me from finding his stance reasonable, and it's the kind of reason that is hard to simply refute.

## What is He Protecting?

Tatsuro Yamashita staying off streaming media is not because he doesn't understand the internet, nor is he a stodgy traditionalist. He actually uses Spotify to listen to others' music all the time, and says he often browses the global TOP50 to feel the vibe of contemporary popular music. He just doesn't want to put his own work in it.

To understand this, you first have to know what level of "control freak" he is—and this is meant as a compliment. For his recordings, he often single-handedly handles the lead vocals, backing vocals, guitar, percussion, and synthesizer, and his production standards are incredibly strict.

> "When I make a record, from the first note to the last note, it is my decision. Putting it in a place where an algorithm can interrupt, skip, or shuffle it at any time... to me, that is not listening to music, that is consuming music."

What he protects is not just royalties, but the integrity of an experience. To him, an album is a single entity. It has a beginning, an end, a breathing rhythm, and something he wants you to feel. The logic of streaming media is to break this entity apart, turning it into individual pieces of "content," and letting an algorithm decide what you listen to next.

The conflict between these two things is not commercial, but philosophical.

## Streaming is Not the Original Sin

After talking about Tatsuro Yamashita, let's talk about the other side.

How much of this City Pop revival relies on streaming media and video platforms? Even Tatsuro Yamashita's own name is widely known among young people outside Japan largely because his songs appear in various lo-fi mixes, cover videos, and NetEase Cloud playlists (even if those are unauthorized versions).

That is to say, his influence has, in a sense, been amplified by the very thing he resists the most.

Think about it: if no one searched for his "RIDE ON TIME" on Bilibili or YouTube, if those making City Pop playlists didn't push his name to algorithms, how many people would take the trouble to buy his CDs today? The blockade of copyright sometimes relies on the spread of piracy to maintain its heat. This paradox is hard to resolve.

The greatest contribution of streaming media, after all, is lowering the barrier for "hearing something for the first time." In the past, if you wanted to hear a niche Japanese City Pop record, you had to rely on friends, record stores, or luck. Now, if an algorithm recommends it to you one day, you can immediately click play. No matter how you look at it, this is a real benefit for the dissemination of music, and I myself have discovered a lot of music this way.

## What Has Streaming Changed?

Since the advent of streaming media, fewer people listen to an album from start to finish. It's not that they are unwilling, but the concept itself has gradually faded. You swipe your finger, or the algorithm automatically switches, and then you are on to another song. That experience of an album as a cohesive whole has slowly disappeared. Of course, when I browse my daily recommendations now, I still subconsciously click into the album page, save it, and then listen to it all the way through.

This is not entirely a bad thing. Singles have their own meaning, and many eras in the history of popular music revolved around singles. But it has indeed changed the way we build a relationship with music. In the past, you would listen to a record repeatedly until you could hum every track and memorize every transition, and that record became tied to a specific period of your life. Now, it's more often "this song is pretty good," adding it to a playlist, and then forgetting it.

## To Stream or Not to Stream?

I don't think Tatsuro Yamashita's choice is the only correct answer, but I also don't think he's just playing the big shot. His choices are consistent: no TV appearances, no massive stadium shows, no music videos (though he softened on this later). All of this, taken together, constitutes a complete stance as a creator.

For most musicians, being on streaming platforms is almost a non-choice. Not being on them is equivalent to not existing in the modern world. If a newcomer announced today, "I only sell CDs," the result would most likely be that nobody knows them, rather than becoming an avant-garde artist. Tatsuro Yamashita can make this choice because he is already Tatsuro Yamashita. His name itself is traffic, and his very existence creates a sense of scarcity.

This logic cannot be replicated or universalized.

However, from our perspective as listeners, behind the convenience of streaming media lies a hidden cost: we are slowly losing the habit of "taking a song seriously." Not because we are lazy, but because the system is designed to keep you swiping forward.

Listening to an album from the first song to the last has nothing to do with ritual; it is simply a way to slow time down. It is more profound, more like a sincere conversation.

Streaming has given us the world's music library. But if an infinite library ultimately makes you unable to seriously read a single book to the end, that is quite a pity.

If you want to listen to his music legitimately, buying physical CDs is the way.
